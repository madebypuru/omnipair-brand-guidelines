Section 01

Brand Character

Who Omnipair is. Everything in this guide flows from here.

→ Voice Principles → Tone Calibration

Before a single word is written, understand who Omnipair is. The brand prism gives us six dimensions — each one has a direct voice implication.

The Omnipair Person — your north star
"A sharp, self-assured architect in their early 30s. Shows up in a Porsche wearing a well-cut jacket and unbranded designer pants — premium, but no logos. Calm and precise, never loud. Has strong opinions about how on-chain finance should work and voices them as reasoned arguments, not insults. A quiet rebel rejecting the status quo through what they build rather than how loudly they complain. Doesn't ask for your trust — would rather you verify everything yourself. Ships only what matters."

The six dimensions

Physique — How Omnipair looks
Technical · Refined · Supreme
Copy should feel precise and considered. Never rough, never padded. Made by builders, presented with taste.
Personality — How Omnipair enters a room
Rational · Confident · Visionary
Make claims through logic, not enthusiasm. Conviction without chest-beating. Decisions through reason.
Culture — What Omnipair believes
Inventive · Ownership · Transparency
Say what something actually does. No vague promises. Transparency is demonstrated, not claimed.
Relationship — What Omnipair promises
Control · Reliability · Opportunity
Promise access and dependable systems — not outcomes. Doesn't guarantee results, guarantees the infrastructure.
Reflection — Who Omnipair attracts
Strategists · Long-Term Thinkers · Early Adopters
Write for people who read carefully and think in systems. They will fact-check you. Be glad when they do.
Self-Image — How users feel
Independent · Confident · Capable
The reader should feel smarter after reading. Never patronize. The user chose this — the copy should affirm that instinct.
Section 02

Voice Principles

Five rules that govern how Omnipair speaks across every medium.

These aren't stylistic preferences — they're structural rules that follow directly from the prism. Every piece of copy should be tested against all five.

Precision over volume
Say the exact thing, not the approximate thing. Omnipair writes like someone who has thought carefully before opening their mouth. Fewer words, more weight.
Avoid
"Omnipair is an incredibly powerful platform that helps traders and DeFi users unlock their full potential with cutting-edge technology and real-time insights."
↓
Use instead
"Omnipair gives you the infrastructure to trade on-chain without asking permission. Precise execution. No intermediaries."
Logic, not hype
Omnipair makes arguments, not announcements. Every claim should be supportable. If you can't point to what makes it true, don't say it.
Avoid
"The most revolutionary DeFi protocol ever built. Join thousands of users experiencing the future of finance."
↓
Use instead
"Built for non-custodial execution at institutional speed. The architecture is open — read it, verify it, run it."
Confidence without noise
Omnipair is certain of what it's building. That certainty doesn't need to be performed. Avoid exclamation marks, superlatives, and intensity words. The conviction is in the substance.
Transparency as a value, not a feature
Don't market openness — demonstrate it. Write like someone who would rather you look at the code than take their word for it. Invite scrutiny. Don't promise trust, make trust beside the point.
Avoid
"You can trust Omnipair. We're committed to full transparency and user safety."
↓
Use instead
"The contracts are open. The execution is on-chain. Trust is optional — verification is built in."
Quiet rebellion, not loud critique
Omnipair rejects the current system by building something better, not by naming what's wrong with competitors. Never punch at rivals. The product is the argument.
Avoid
"Unlike [Competitor], we don't charge hidden fees or lock your assets."
↓
Use instead
"Your assets remain yours. Every fee is visible before execution. That's the whole model."
Section 03

Tone Calibration

The same person, speaking in different rooms. The character stays constant — the register adjusts.

Omnipair's tone doesn't shift wildly by context. Think of it as one person who knows which room they're in — never performing, always calibrated.

Formal ↔ Casual
Clinical
Colloquial

Professional and precise, but not stiff. Technical vocabulary used correctly, not wielded to impress.

Warm ↔ Cold
Friendly
Distant

Slightly cool. Not cold — there's respect for the reader. But Omnipair doesn't try to be likable. It tries to be useful and correct.

Certain ↔ Tentative
Declarative
Uncertain

Very certain. Omnipair doesn't "try to" or "hope to." Weasel words undermine credibility. State the thing.

Audience modes

The depth of technical language and the nature of the argument shifts by reader. Know who you're writing for first.

The Strategist
Long-term thinker
Cares about system architecture, protocol integrity, five-year trajectory. Dense is fine. Make the argument fully. Technical precision.
The Trader
Execution-focused
Cares about speed, fees, slippage, control. High conviction, low patience for abstraction. Lead with function. Numbers over narrative.
The Early Adopter
Curious and forward-looking
Wants to be early to something real. Not easily impressed, wants signal not noise. Show your thinking, not just your product.
What they all share
Sophisticated readers
None of them responds well to being sold at. Write for people who will fact-check you — and be glad when they do.

Section 05

Dos & Don'ts

Run any piece of copy through both columns before publishing.

Do
✓Lead with what the product does, not how it feels
✓Use specific numbers when available (latency, uptime, fees)
✓Write in the present tense — Omnipair does, not will do
✓Make arguments that could be refuted — they earn trust
✓Let the reader reach their own conclusion from your facts
✓Use short sentences for impact, longer ones for explanation
✓Acknowledge complexity — don't oversimplify
✓Write headlines that are claims, not slogans
✓Invite verification — point to the code, the contract, the data
Don't
✕Use exclamation marks in product copy
✕Promise outcomes you can't guarantee
✕Name or shade competitors, even subtly
✕Use urgency tactics ("limited time", "don't miss out")
✕Add modifiers that add no information ("truly", "really", "very")
✕Start with "We believe" or "We think" — just state it
✕Use passive voice to avoid accountability
✕Pad body copy — if a sentence earns nothing, cut it
✕Announce the announcement ("excited to share…")

The 10 rules — fast QA checklist

When in doubt, run your copy against these. If it fails more than one, rewrite it.

  1. 01 Start with the thing, not the feeling about the thing. "On-chain execution" before "a seamless experience."
  2. 02 Cut every word that doesn't earn its place. If removing it changes nothing, remove it.
  3. 03 Never claim to be "the best" — demonstrate it. Superlatives are for the reader to conclude, not the writer to assert.
  4. 04 Write in the present tense. Omnipair does, not plans to do or hopes to do.
  5. 05 No exclamation marks in product copy. If the copy needs one to feel exciting, the copy isn't strong enough.
  6. 06 Invite verification. If you can point to the code, the contract, or the data — do it.
  7. 07 Don't compete out loud. The product competes. The copy doesn't need to.
  8. 08 Use technical language correctly or not at all. Jargon that's wrong is worse than no jargon.
  9. 09 Make one argument per paragraph. "Additionally" is usually a signal to split.
  10. 10 Read it as your most skeptical user. If they'd roll their eyes, rewrite it. If they'd nod, ship it.
Section 06

Copy Templates

Ready-to-use structures for common contexts. Fill in the specifics — the voice is already set.

Hero Headline
[Specific capability], without [the thing it removes]. e.g. "On-chain execution at institutional speed, without the intermediary." State the real capability + name what it replaces or eliminates. No adjectives in the lead.
Hero Subheadline
[What you actually get] + [why it's different in one clause]. e.g. "Infrastructure designed for non-custodial trading. The architecture is open — read it, verify it, run it." Two short sentences work better than one long one. The second sentence does the work.
Feature Description
[Feature name] — [one-line functional description]. e.g. "Non-custodial routing — your positions stay on-chain, every step of the execution." No marketing prefix. Name it, define it, done. Resist the urge to explain why it matters — show it in the product.
UI Error / Status Message
State what happened. Say what to do next. e.g. "Transaction failed — insufficient liquidity in this pool at this size. Try a smaller position or a different pair." No apologies. No vagueness. Name the reason, provide the path forward.
Social — Announcement
[What was built]. [Why it matters in one sentence]. [Where to verify it.] e.g. "Omnipair now supports [X]. It means [concrete change for the user]. Contracts here: [link]." Never announce that you're announcing. No "excited to share." Start with the thing.
Social — Thought Piece
Make a specific, arguable claim. Support it. Don't hedge the conclusion. e.g. "Most liquidity routing optimizes for the protocol's fee capture, not the trader's execution. Here's how the incentives are structured and why they produce this outcome: [thread]." The point of view is the value. The argument is the proof. If the take isn't arguable, it's not interesting.
Long-Form / Docs Opening
State the problem precisely. Then say what this document addresses. e.g. "Non-custodial execution across fragmented liquidity sources introduces three routing challenges. This document covers how Omnipair handles each." Skip the preamble. Start with the problem, not with what you're about to explain.
